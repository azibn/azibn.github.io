## Research

In recent discoveries, there are a small number of stars that have shown unique dips in their transits caused by analogues of comets in our Solar system. The most convincing evidence of this so far is that of Beta Pic (Zieba et al. 2019). Similar systems such as KIC 3542116 and KIC 11084727 (Rappaport et al. 2018) also boost us with substantial motivation to hunt for exocomets. The significance of evidence for exocomets will allow us to understand the early stage formation and evolution of extrasolar systems, and further trace back any water/ice compositions within those systems.

A simple automated method to detect comet-like transits was developed by Kennedy et al. 2018, which used Kepler data in the main analysis. As newer missions such as TESS will provide more data about younger stars (a likely host for exocomets) with various astrophysical variability, further development of this algorithm is essential, and is the current part of this PhD.

My other research interests include the search for faint binary systems with spectroscopic data. This stems from my MSc thesis on exploring Bayesian techniques, using PolyChord nested sampling, towards the development of the Gaia DR4 pipeline with Dr George Seabroke and Dr Pablo Lemos.
