In recent discoveries, there are a small number of stars that have shown unique dimming behaviour by what is thought to be caused by analogues of comets in our Solar system. The most convincing evidence of these dips so far is that of Beta Pic ([Zieba et al. 2019](https://arxiv.org/abs/1903.11071)). Similar star systems such as KIC 3542116 and KIC 11084727 ([Rappaport et al. 2018](https://arxiv.org/abs/1708.06069)) also boost us with substantial motivation to hunt for exocomets. The significance of evidence for exocomets will allow us to understand the early stage formation and evolution of extrasolar systems. Could there be evidence of water/ice from the origins (e.g: exoplanets) of these exocomets that signal the possibility of a habitable environment?

To hunt for these exocomets, automated methods are the main method of detection, with the main motivation of it being less error prone in comparison to by-eye methods, and allowing us to test specific hyppotheses more robustly over a wide range of data. A "simple" automated method to detect comet-like transits was developed by [Kennedy et al. 2018](https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2019MNRAS.482.5587K/abstract), which was successful in retrieving interesting information using [Kepler](https://www.nasa.gov/mission_pages/kepler/main/index.html) data in the main analysis. As newer missions such as [TESS](https://www.nasa.gov/tess-transiting-exoplanet-survey-satellite) will provide more data to us about younger stars (a likely host for exocomets) with various astrophysical variability, further development of this pipeline is essential, and is the current scope of the PhD. By expanding the work to account for newer data, we can determine the nature of these exocomets, their origins, and their implications to exoplanetary astronomy.
